What is FakenewsResearch

Disinformation is rampant on social media, and researchers from all around the world are studying this problem from a broad variety of different perspecives. Especially after 2016, the scientific production about all kinds of information disorders has exploded. We now are flooded by thousands of new publications per year. Among all these contributions, it's sometime hard to keep track of the most valuable ones, or the latest out.

We wanted an aggregator of scientific publications that user can seek for retrieving the most influential works, for discovering new releases, a place where to find a comprehensive collection of papers about the topic.

FakenewsResearch is an automated collection of scientific papers, based on the data retrieved from the Microsoft Academic Graph APIs. Also, FakenewsResearch doesn't allow you to only search for title - there's plenty of search engines online. Wee wanted to guide the search of a paper: we highlighted different types of influence, we suggested similar papers, we let you search by tag or by author. We plan to automatically retrieve subfields, and tag papers by subfield, putting a bit of order in the vast and confuse production on this subject.

How does FakenewsResearch work?

We downloaded any paper that contains one of these keywords in the title:

  • fake news
  • misinformation
  • disinformation
  • hoax
  • false news
  • social bot
  • fact check%
  • fact-check%
  • infodemic
  • information disorder

We are aware that we may have downloaded many unrelevant papers, and perhaps missed many relevant contributions, but as long as we can provide a useful service, we don't think a few misplaced papers are a too big issue.

Each paper comes with a list of papers it references. We download them all, as they may be relevant papers without one of the keywords in the title. We flag all the papers containing one of the keyword in the title or in the abstract as relevant, and we flag the remaining papers as "referenced papers". When you query about a word, you search only the relevant papers, unless you check the box search also in referenced papers. We then built a citation network, and we computed several centrality measures like the betweenness centrality, the pagerank, and the hubs and authorities centrality. We believe these measure to give different ideas of influence of a paper: some papers receive many authorable citations, and we can consider them as fundamental inspirations for important works; some other papers cite many authorable works, hence they are valuable (also) for their rich refecences section; some papers are in between od two groups of papers, like bridges in the middle of two disciplines. We highlighted the hidden gems in this dataset, among the query results.

Last, we computed a text similarity score for each possible pair of papers, using Doc2Vec. This allowed us to save, for each paper, the 10 most similar to check. We believe that even a basic recommendation system like this one may help researchers to find interesting contributions to read, moving from one paper to another by following suggestions.
We update the dataset and repeat all the analyses twice per month, to keep up with the enormous scientific production of these years.

Hey, metadata about my paper is wrong!

You may experience some bug here and there. Maybe some papers have no abstract, or the references list doesn't match with the one you can find elsewhere, or the author list may be partial, with wrong affiliations. We are not responsible for the data itself, but we also believe that people at Microsoft are doing a terrific job, and we should just thank them. All this application is possible because of them sharing their hard work with the community, and we are extremely grateful. If you see something wrong, please contact us, and we will try to fix it based on your alerts.

The same applies, of course, to our analyses and algorithms. FakenewsResearch is updated automatically! It means we don't do any supervision, we only check if everything works after the last update. If something gets messed up, please let us know.

About us

We are Alfonso Semeraro and Giancarlo Ruffo from the Computer Science Department of the University of Turin, also members of the Applied Research on Computational Complex Systems group. Among other projects, we work on issues related to information disorders online: we monitor the spread of low credibility news on social networks, and we study how pervasive are such contents on the debate about sensitive topics, like immigration or vaccines.
